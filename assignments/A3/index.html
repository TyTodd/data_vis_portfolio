Final Visualization

My initial attempts for this visualization aimed to strike a balance between readability and visual impact. I wanted to create something that clearly communicated the trends in COVID-19 case counts over time while still maintaining some of the cyclical patterns emphasized in the original New York Times spiral chart. The first iterations explored different ways to represent the data—starting with a traditional line graph that mapped cases over time and then moving to a segmented bar chart that broke cases into discrete time periods. These versions made it easier to track overall trends, but they lacked the intuitive structure that highlighted how case surges built upon one another.

For my final visualization, I used the Gantt chart feature in Tableau, structuring the data so that months were displayed as columns and years as rows, with both set as discrete fields. I then dragged the SUM of cumulative cases onto the size mark, allowing the bubble sizes to reflect case counts. This approach closely aligns with the original intent of the NYT visualization while enhancing clarity. The calendar-like layout is intuitive to most audiences, making it easy to follow both chronological progression and seasonal cycles of COVID-19 surges. A key advantage of this format is that it allows direct comparisons between months across different years—showing how October, November, and December 2021 led into the massive Omicron surge of January 2022, while also providing a clear view of how January case counts compared across 2020, 2021, and 2022. Ultimately, this visualization provides a stronger blend of clarity and insight, ensuring that both trends and seasonality are communicated effectively.
